# Luigi Footprints Foundation

## Protecting Wildlife by Empowering Communities

### A future where people and nature thrive together

In the heart of Kenya, where elephants still roam ancient migration routes and communities have lived alongside wildlife for generations, the future of both people and nature is deeply connected.

At the Luigi Footprints Foundation, we believe conservation is not simply about protecting wildlife. It is about creating opportunities, restoring dignity, and ensuring that communities have the resources they need to build sustainable futures.

Because when communities thrive, wildlife thrives too.

Every tree planted, every student supported, every woman empowered, and every wildlife corridor restored brings us one step closer to a future where people and nature can flourish together.

---

## Our Story

The Luigi Footprints Foundation was established in honour of Luigi Francescon, whose love for Kenya, its wildlife, and its people shaped the vision that continues to guide our work today.

Growing up near Nairobi National Park, Luigi experienced a Kenya where wildlife moved freely across the landscape and where nature was an everyday part of life. Those early experiences instilled in him a lifelong respect for the natural world and a deep concern for the wellbeing of future generations.

He also understood that conservation and human development are inseparable. Wildlife cannot survive if communities are struggling. Equally, communities cannot thrive if the ecosystems they depend upon are lost.

Following his passing in 2016, the foundation was created to carry forward his vision: protecting wildlife while creating opportunities for children, families, and communities across Kenya.

Today, that vision lives on through practical, community-led conservation programs that create lasting impact for both people and nature.

---

## Our Philosophy

### Dignity for Communities is Equal to Protection for Wildlife

This simple belief guides everything we do.

For too long, conservation has often been viewed as something separate from people's everyday lives. Our experience has shown the opposite.

Communities living closest to wildlife are not obstacles to conservation—they are its greatest partners.

When young people receive education, they become future conservation leaders.

When women have economic opportunities, families become more resilient.

When landscapes are restored, wildlife and communities both benefit.

When people feel included, respected, and empowered, conservation becomes sustainable.

This is the foundation of our work.

# What We Do

## Educating the Next Generation of Conservation Leaders

The future of conservation depends on the young people who will inherit it.

Across the Amboseli ecosystem and surrounding communities, we invest in education, leadership development, and environmental awareness programs that help young people discover their potential and understand their role in protecting the world around them.

Through scholarships, conservation workshops, mentorship, and creative arts initiatives, we are helping nurture a generation of informed, confident, and compassionate leaders.

Our programs have reached thousands of students, providing opportunities to learn about wildlife, environmental stewardship, leadership, and community responsibility.

One of our most inspiring initiatives is the Amboseli OYC Choir, where young people use music, dance, and poetry to share messages of conservation, culture, and coexistence. Through creativity and storytelling, they become powerful ambassadors for change within their communities.

---

## Restoring Landscapes, One Tree at a Time

Healthy ecosystems support healthy communities.

Through our Olchani Project, we are restoring degraded landscapes, strengthening wildlife corridors, and helping communities become active stewards of their environment.

Tree planting is not simply about putting seedlings in the ground. It is about rebuilding ecosystems, improving climate resilience, creating livelihoods, and securing resources for future generations.

Working alongside local communities, we have established community-managed nurseries capable of producing tens of thousands of seedlings while creating employment and skills development opportunities for women and youth.

Every tree represents hope, resilience, and a commitment to a better future.

---

## Creating Opportunities for Communities

Conservation succeeds when people can see tangible benefits in their daily lives.

That is why we invest in initiatives that improve livelihoods while supporting environmental protection.

From women's enterprises and community businesses to conservation-linked income opportunities, our programs help families build sustainable sources of income without compromising the natural resources around them.

The Dignity Tailors Program supports women through skills development, craftsmanship, and entrepreneurship.

Community beadwork initiatives help Maasai women access fair markets and retain more value from their traditional crafts.

Tourism partnerships create opportunities for visitors to participate directly in conservation activities while supporting local livelihoods.

By strengthening economic resilience, we help communities become active partners in protecting wildlife and natural ecosystems.

---

## Helping People and Wildlife Coexist

Living alongside wildlife can bring both opportunity and challenge.

For communities near protected areas, encounters with predators and other wildlife can threaten livelihoods and create conflict.

Rather than choosing between people and wildlife, we focus on solutions that support both.

Our human-wildlife coexistence initiatives include predator deterrent lighting systems that help protect livestock and families, as well as community beekeeping projects that generate income while discouraging wildlife from entering settlements.

These practical solutions reduce conflict, improve safety, and strengthen support for conservation at the community level.

---

## Supporting the People Who Protect Wildlife

Behind every protected landscape are dedicated men and women working tirelessly to keep wildlife safe.

Wildlife rangers spend long periods away from their families, often in remote and challenging conditions.

Through our Dignity Housing Project, we are improving living conditions for rhino rangers in Nairobi National Park by providing safe, self-contained housing units equipped with essential facilities.

These homes are more than buildings. They are a recognition of the sacrifices made by those who dedicate their lives to protecting Kenya's wildlife.

By caring for wildlife guardians, we strengthen conservation efforts on the ground.

---

## Responding When Nature Needs Us Most

Conservation requires long-term commitment, but it also demands action during times of crisis.

During severe drought periods, we partnered with wildlife authorities to provide emergency water and hay for wildlife struggling to survive.

We have supported efforts to remove invasive plant species threatening ecosystems and worked alongside communities to restore degraded habitats.

When challenges arise, we respond where help is needed most.

# Our Impact

Every project we undertake is built around a simple idea: lasting conservation happens when communities are empowered to lead it.

Through education, restoration, community development, and wildlife protection, we are helping create:

- Stronger and more resilient communities
- Increased opportunities for women and young people
- Healthier ecosystems and restored habitats
- Reduced human-wildlife conflict
- Greater awareness and environmental stewardship
- Better protection for wildlife and the people who safeguard it

The impact extends far beyond numbers.

It can be seen in a student pursuing their education, a family earning a sustainable income, a ranger returning to a safe home, and a landscape beginning to recover.

These are the footprints we hope to leave behind.
